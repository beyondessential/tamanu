# BES proposed solution — Zimbabwe National Shared Health Record

_Draft narrative for the UNDP/MoHCC bid. Expert-voice prose, ready for Jane to shape into the technical proposal. Australian/NZ spelling. An internal notes section at the end flags where we are stretching ahead of what ships today._

## The proposed solution

BES proposes to establish Zimbabwe's National Shared Health Record on **Tamanu**, a free and open-source health platform already operating as the national health record in six countries. Rather than building a new repository from scratch, the Ministry adopts a proven, production system and configures it to the role the Terms of Reference describe: a centralised, authoritative, longitudinal record of every citizen's care, and the single source of truth for patient identity across the health system.

Architecturally, Tamanu sits at the centre of the Digital Health Exchange as the **Patient Master Index and OpenHIE Client Registry**. Every connected system — iMpilo, eLMIS, LIMS, DHIS2, and the private-sector EMRs such as Bahmni, Trimed and omnisol — resolves patient identity against Tamanu and reads and writes clinical data through a single, standards-based interface, instead of each system integrating directly with every other. This is the core of the Ministry's own diagnosis in the ToR: point-to-point integration does not scale. A hub-and-spoke exchange replaces the N² tangle of bilateral links with one connection per system to the central registries — client, facility, organisation, terminology — through which any authorised system can reach any other's data. It is the difference between a road between every pair of towns and a national highway every town joins once.

The exchange interface is a **HL7 FHIR R4 API**. This is not a specification we intend to build for Zimbabwe — it runs in Tamanu production today, materialising patient, encounter, diagnosis, medication, immunisation, laboratory, observation and practitioner data as conformant FHIR resources. We publish the integration profiles, stand up a public sandbox against which vendors can develop and self-certify, and gate access to the live environment behind an authorisation process. The heavy lifting of each EMR integration therefore sits with the EMR, working to documentation and a test harness we provide — which is what makes a foundational deployment achievable inside the contract window while the Ministry rolls out connections over time. Very few systems anywhere in the region offer a FHIR R4 API in genuine production; this is a concrete, demonstrable differentiator against the open-standards evaluation criteria.

Because Tamanu is a complete clinical system, the SHR ships with the clinical surface the ToR asks for, not just a database: a chronological longitudinal patient view, allergies and problem lists surfaced at the point of care, immunisation and growth records for child health, laboratory results with reference ranges, and drug–allergy and interaction checking. Patients reach their own record through the **Tamanu patient portal**, and can share a standards-based summary of their record — Tamanu already implements the **International Patient Summary** — for referrals, cross-border care, or their own use. Clinical staff who need to see the consolidated national record use the same Tamanu interface already in production, so there is no bespoke middleware or new front-end to build and maintain.

## Centralised cloud vs distributed — our recommendation

The ToR asks the question implicitly; we answer it explicitly. **We recommend a single, centrally-hosted cloud SHR as the default**, with all access and consent rules enforced at the FHIR API layer. This keeps the model simple and safe: data governance lives in one place, sensitive-data segmentation and role-based access are evaluated on every request against the Health Worker Registry, and patient consent — the elegant, patient-owned model where a provider requests access to specific parts of a record and the patient grants it, comparable to a third-party app-authorisation flow — is expressed as an API permission rather than woven into data replication. This directly addresses the hardest problem the ToR raises around HIV, mental health and other sensitive data in a country with very high HIV prevalence, without the failure modes that sank comparable efforts elsewhere.

Tamanu's distinctive strength, however, is its **offline-capable synchronisation**: the same platform can run at the facility level and stay in service through the unreliable connectivity that characterises much of the network. We therefore propose the synchronising, facility-server deployment as an **optional model for sites where online access is a genuine barrier** — the SHR benefit that no purely cloud-hosted or FHIR-server competitor can offer — while being clear that consent and access control remain enforced at the API boundary in every configuration, and that this option carries additional infrastructure and support cost. The Ministry gets a pragmatic centralised backbone today and a credible path to resilient, distributed operation where it is warranted.

## Why BES, and the barriers this overcomes

The solution is open-source end to end (Tamanu, PostgreSQL, containerised deployment), so there is no proprietary lock-in; source code, documentation and training transfer full ownership to the MoHCC, satisfying the sustainability and skills-transfer criteria directly. It is built to the standards the ToR names — FHIR R4, OpenHIE, IHE profiles — and Tamanu carries robust **patient matching, deduplication and record merge**, the foundation of a trustworthy unique patient identifier and the antidote to the duplicate, inconsistent records the fragmented landscape has produced today. In short: a system proven at national scale, an interoperability layer already running in production, and a deployment model that meets Zimbabwe where its infrastructure actually is.
